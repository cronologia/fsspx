Chronology of the Society of Saint Pius X (1970–present)

An open, source-referenced chronology of the Fraternidade Sacerdotal São Pio X (Society of Saint Pius X, SSPX/FSSPX) — the traditionalist Catholic priestly society founded by Archbishop Marcel Lefebvre in 1970 — and its relationship with the Holy See.

Divisions timeline

How the groups around the SSPX forked over time, at a glance. Each fork is summarized with citations below the figure; contested characterizations are attributed there. One lane is not a split in the strict sense: the Campos priests' union was an organizationally separate Brazilian ally of the SSPX, and its lane ends where it was regularized by Rome (2002); the 2026 lane marks the rupture between the SSPX and Rome (a 'schism' per the DDF decree, disputed by the SSPX), not the departure of a group from the Society.

About

This is a work-in-progress dataset compiled from public sources (see references). The Society's canonical status is contested and time-sensitive: characterizations are attributed to their authors (the Holy See, the SSPX itself, commentators), never asserted in the site's own voice, and dates not yet verified against a primary source are flagged with a ? mark. Corrections against primary sources are welcome.

What it is
An international priestly society of the traditionalist Catholic movement, dedicated to the formation of priests and the preservation of the traditional Latin Mass (1962 Missal) and pre-conciliar doctrine and practice.[1]
Founded
1 November 1970, Fribourg, Switzerland — erected as a pia unio ad experimentum by Bishop François Charrière of Lausanne, Geneva and Fribourg.[1]
Founder
Archbishop Marcel Lefebvre (1905–1991), former Archbishop of Dakar and Superior General of the Holy Ghost Fathers.[3]
Headquarters
Menzingen, Switzerland; mother seminary at Écône (Valais).[1]
Scale (self-reported, January 2026)
733 priests, 264 seminarians, 5 seminaries (Écône, Dillwyn, Flavigny, Zaitzkofen, La Reja), 760 Mass centers in 77 countries. Lay-adherent estimates range from ~100,000 (independent estimates) to ~600,000 (SSPX's own figure). ?[1]
In Brazil
Casa Autônoma do Brasil ('district in formation') since 2017, previously under the Argentina-based District of South America; Brazilian seminarians are formed at La Reja, Argentina.[22] [21]
Canonical status
Time-sensitive and contested: approved 1970–1975; canonically irregular from 1975, though repeatedly described by Vatican officials as not formally schismatic; since the 2 July 2026 DDF decree, the Holy See describes the Society's clergy as in schism — a decree the SSPX rejects as invalid.[4] [24] [26]

Chronology

Key events in chronological order. A ? flag marks dates not yet verified against a primary source.

1970s
1970 1970-11-01 Fribourg, Switzerland Founding of the SSPX — Bishop François Charrière erects the Society as a pia unio on an ad experimentum basis for six years; Marcel Lefebvre is founder and superior.[1]
1971 1971 Écône, Valais, Switzerland Écône seminary opens — The International Seminary of Saint Pius X receives its first cohort of 24 candidates; 32 more enter in October 1972.[1]
1974 1974-11-11/13 Écône Canonical visitation — Two Belgian priests inspect the seminary on behalf of a commission of cardinals.[1]
1974 1974-11-21 Écône Lefebvre's Declaration — Lefebvre professes adherence to 'eternal Rome' while rejecting 'the Rome of neo-modernist tendencies' — the text that became the Society's ideological charter. The date is verified against the official text published by the SSPX General House, dated 21 November 1974.[49] [1]
1975 1975-05-06 Fribourg Approval withdrawn — Bishop Pierre Mamie withdraws the Society's pia unio status with the approval of the Congregation for Religious and Paul VI; the SSPX disputes the legality of the procedure.[1]
1976 1976-01-11 El Palmar de Troya, Spain The Palmar consecrations — an Écône personnel link — Fr. Maurice Revaz, canon of the Grand-Saint-Bernard and canon-law professor at Écône, drives Archbishop Ngô Đình Thục from Italy to El Palmar de Troya (arriving Christmas Eve 1975). On 11 January 1976 Thục consecrates five Palmarian bishops — declared excommunicated by the nuncio to Spain on 15 January — and on 28 January Revaz himself is consecrated a Palmarian bishop by Clemente Domínguez and others, placing him in the Palmarian, not the Thục, lineage; he leaves the movement about two years later (Lundberg). Lefebvre took no part; testimony reported by Lundberg has him declining an invitation to Palmar and pointing the group to Thục — attributed testimony, not established fact.[57] [33] [31]
1976 1976-06-29 Écône The 'hot summer' ordinations — Lefebvre ordains priests despite an explicit prohibition, after being publicly rebuked by Paul VI in consistory on 24 May.[1]
1976 1976-07-22 Rome Suspension a divinis — After a 1 July suspension from conferring orders and continued refusal to comply, Lefebvre is suspended from all sacramental ministry.[1]
1977 1977 Écône Guérard des Lauriers dismissed from Écône — Fr. Michel-Louis Guérard des Lauriers OP, professor of theology at the seminary from 1971, is dismissed in autumn 1977 after teaching the vacant-see thesis to seminarians in a September retreat despite Lefebvre's prohibition — the SSPX account (Si Si No No, 1998) and his sedeprivationist biographer (IMBC) agree on the dismissal and its ground. Consecrated by Thục in 1981, he became the source of the sedeprivationist ('Cassiciacum') lineages — see the episcopal genealogy.[59] [60]
1978 1978-11 ? Rome Lefebvre meets John Paul II — Shortly after the papal election, Lefebvre seeks regularization; talks continue intermittently through the 1980s.[3]
1980s
1980 1980 Buenos Aires / La Reja, Argentina Latin American seminary founded — The Seminario Nuestra Señora Corredentora is founded in the Buenos Aires priory and moves to La Reja in 1981 — the formation house for Latin America, including Brazil.[12]
1982 1982 Switzerland Schmidberger succeeds Lefebvre — Fr. Franz Schmidberger becomes Superior General (1982–1994); Lefebvre remains the Society's episcopal head.[1]
1983 1983 United States The 'Nine' leave — SSPV founded — Fr. Clarence Kelly and eight US priests are expelled from or leave the SSPX in a dispute over the 1962 Missal and openness to sedevacantism, founding the Society of Saint Pius V — a distinct, sedevacantist-leaning body.[10]
1987 1987-06 Écône Consecrations announced — Lefebvre announces his intention to consecrate bishops without a papal mandate, citing his age and the need to continue ordinations.[1]
1988 1988-05-05 Rome Protocol signed — and withdrawn — Lefebvre and Cardinal Ratzinger sign a protocol of agreement on 5 May. On 6 May Lefebvre writes to Ratzinger announcing he will proceed with episcopal consecrations by 30 June (letter text published by SSPX Asia); 'Ecclesia Dei' treats his letter of 2 June 1988 as the definitive refusal.[1] [50] [13]
1988 1988-06-30 Écône The Écône consecrations — Lefebvre, with Bishop Antônio de Castro Mayer (emeritus of Campos, Brazil) as co-consecrator, consecrates Bernard Fellay, Bernard Tissier de Mallerais, Richard Williamson and Alfonso de Galarreta without papal mandate.[1]
1988 1988-07-01 Rome Excommunications declared — The Congregation for Bishops declares that Lefebvre, Castro Mayer and the four new bishops incurred latae sententiae excommunication.[1] [13]
1988 1988-07-02 Rome 'Ecclesia Dei' — John Paul II's motu proprio calls the consecrations 'a schismatic act' and establishes the Ecclesia Dei Commission for faithful attached to the traditional liturgy.[13]
1988 1988-07-18 Hauterive Abbey, Switzerland FSSP founded — Twelve former SSPX priests led by Fr. Josef Bisig found the Priestly Fraternity of Saint Peter in full communion with Rome.[8]
1988 1988-08-15 Campos, Brazil Campos priests form the Priestly Union of Saint John Mary Vianney — The priests loyal to Bishop Castro Mayer formally organize the União Sacerdotal São João Maria Vianney, continuing the traditional Mass across the diocese of Campos.[30]
1990s
1991 1991-03-25 Martigny, Switzerland Death of Marcel Lefebvre — The founder dies at 85 and is buried at the Écône seminary.[3]
1991 1991-04-25 Campos, Brazil Death of Antônio de Castro Mayer — The co-consecrator of the 1988 Écône consecrations dies a month after Lefebvre. Source conflict recorded: Wikipedia (EN/PT) and the SSPX obituary date the death 25 April 1991; catholic-hierarchy.org gives 26 April 1991.[28] [55]
1991 1991-07-28 São Fidélis, Brazil Rangel consecrated for the Campos union — Licínio Rangel is consecrated without papal mandate by Bishop Tissier de Mallerais, assisted by de Galarreta and Williamson, to lead the Campos priests after Castro Mayer's death.[29] [30]
1994 1994 Écône Fellay elected Superior General — Bishop Bernard Fellay leads the Society 1994–2018 (re-elected 2006).[1]
2000s
2000 2000-08 ? Rome Jubilee pilgrimage to Rome — Over 5,000 SSPX pilgrims visit the major basilicas; Cardinal Castrillón Hoyos opens contacts, reportedly offering a personal prelature or apostolic administration.[1]
2002 2002-01-18 ? Campos, Brazil Campos priests reconcile with Rome — The priestly union founded by Castro Mayer's followers is received as the Personal Apostolic Administration Saint John Mary Vianney under Bishop Rangel; Fernando Rifan, consecrated in full communion later that year, succeeds him in December — a path the SSPX declined.[30] [36]
2007 2007-07-07 Rome 'Summorum Pontificum' — Benedict XVI liberalizes the 1962 Missal as the 'extraordinary form', meeting one of the SSPX's two stated preconditions for talks.[14]
2009 2009-01 Worldwide Williamson affair — A television interview in which Bishop Richard Williamson denied the Holocaust airs just before the remission is announced, causing worldwide controversy; the SSPX removes him as rector of the La Reja seminary.[5]
2009 2009-01-21 Rome 1988 excommunications remitted — A decree of the Congregation for Bishops (dated 21 January, published 24 January) lifts the excommunications of the four bishops; some sources cite 10 March 2009, the date of Benedict XVI's explanatory letter.[15]
2009 2009-10 ? Vatican Doctrinal talks begin — Discussions between the CDF and the SSPX on Vatican II (religious liberty, ecumenism, collegiality) run until 2012 without producing an agreement.[7]
2010s
2012 2012-10-04 Menzingen Williamson expelled — The SSPX expels Bishop Williamson after unauthorized confirmations in Brazil and public defiance; he goes on to lead the loose 'Resistance' network.[5]
2015 2015-03-19 Nova Friburgo, Brazil Resistance consecration in Brazil — Williamson consecrates Jean-Michel Faure without mandate at the Santa Cruz Monastery, consolidating the 'Resistance' splinter. The local ordinary of Nova Friburgo, Bishop Edney Gouvêa Mattoso, and canonical commentators state that a new latae sententiae excommunication (can. 1382) was thereby incurred; no Holy See statement on the individuals has been located.[5] [56]
2015 2015-09-01 ? Rome Confession faculties for the Jubilee — Pope Francis grants that the faithful can validly and licitly receive absolution from SSPX priests during the Year of Mercy.[16]
2016 2016-03-19 Nova Friburgo, Brazil Second Resistance consecration in Brazil — Williamson consecrates Tomás de Aquino Ferreira da Costa, prior of the Santa Cruz Monastery, without mandate.[35]
2016 2016-11-20 Rome Faculties extended indefinitely — In 'Misericordia et misera' §12 Francis extends the SSPX confession faculty 'until further provisions are made'.[16]
2017 2017-03-19 ? Brazil Casa Autônoma do Brasil — Brazil is erected as an Autonomous House ('district in formation') of the SSPX, separated from the Argentina-based District of South America for linguistic and cultural reasons.[22] [21]
2017 2017-03-27 Rome Marriage provisions — An Ecclesia Dei Commission letter (signed Cardinal Müller) allows local ordinaries to grant faculties so that marriages in the SSPX milieu are celebrated validly.[17]
2017 2017-05-11 Diocese of Arlington, Virginia, USA Zendejas consecrated for the Resistance — Williamson, assisted by Faure and Tomás de Aquino, consecrates Gerardo Zendejas; the local bishop's statement notes the excommunication incurred.[34]
2018 2018-07-11 Écône Pagliarani elected Superior General — The General Chapter elects Fr. Davide Pagliarani, former rector of La Reja, to a 12-year term, succeeding Bishop Fellay.[6]
2020s
2021 2021-07-16 Rome 'Traditionis Custodes' — Francis restricts the 1962 Missal in the mainstream Church. Canonically the motu proprio does not bind the SSPX; press analyses reporting increased attendance at SSPX chapels afterwards are attributed to those analyses.[18] [1]
2022 2022 Cork, Ireland / Poland Later Resistance consecrations — Williamson consecrates Paul Morgan in secret at Cork (14 February 2022), with Giacomo Ballini — whose own private 2021 consecration Williamson announced only in December 2022 — as co-consecrator, and Michał Stobnicki in Poland (15 August 2022), bringing to six the bishops he consecrated for the 'Resistance' between 2015 and 2022.[5]
2024 2024-10-08 Martigny, Switzerland / Écône Death of Tissier de Mallerais — Bishop Bernard Tissier de Mallerais, one of the four 1988 bishops and Lefebvre's biographer, dies in hospital at Martigny (Valais, Switzerland) after a fall at Écône reported in SSPX communiqués; his funeral is celebrated at the Écône seminary on 18 October 2024 and he is buried in its crypt.[51] [52] [53]
2025 2025-01-29 Margate, England Death of Richard Williamson — The expelled 1988 bishop and 'Resistance' leader dies at 84.[27]
2025 2025-05-08 Rome Election of Leo XIV — After the death of Francis (21 April 2025), Robert Prevost is elected pope; his pontificate presides over the 2026 rupture with the SSPX.[4]
2025 2025-08 ? Rome Jubilee-year pilgrimage — The SSPX holds a worldwide pilgrimage to Rome for the 2025 Jubilee.[1]
2026 2026-02-02 Menzingen New consecrations announced — Superior General Pagliarani announces plans to consecrate new bishops on 1 July 2026, citing the age of the Society's remaining bishops and the unresolved doctrinal disagreement.[4] [23]
2026 2026-05-13 Rome DDF warning — Cardinal Víctor Manuel Fernández formally warns that the planned consecrations would constitute 'a schismatic act' entailing excommunication; Pope Leo XIV follows with personal appeals in June.[4] [23]
2026 2026-07-01 Écône The 2026 Écône consecrations — Bishops de Galarreta and Fellay consecrate four new bishops — Pascal Schreiber, Michael Goldade, Michel Poinsinet de Sivry and Marc Hanappier — without papal mandate.[4] [23]
2026 2026-07-02 Rome New excommunications declared — A DDF decree declares the six bishops involved excommunicated latae sententiae, describes SSPX clergy as being in schism, and revokes the 2015–2017 confession and marriage provisions; the SSPX rejects the decree as 'objectively unjust and invalid'. Press summaries differ on how the decree treats priests and laity — each reading is attributed.[24] [25] [26]
2026 2026-07-13 Menzingen / Rome SSPX files canonical recourse — A General House communiqué dated 13 July announces that on 11 July the Society submitted a preliminary recourse to the DDF against the 2 July decree, per cann. 1734 ff. of the Code of Canon Law; the SSPX states that the recourse suspends the decree's execution under can. 1353 — the Society's own characterization, with no Holy See response reported as of 19 July 2026.[38] [39]

Episcopal genealogy

Who consecrated whom. Episcopal genealogy is the clearest way to keep the traditionalist groups distinct: the SSPX and 'Resistance' bishops all descend from Marcel Lefebvre, while the Palmar de Troya (Palmarian) bishops descend from Archbishop Ngô Đình Thục and have no lineage connection to Lefebvre or the SSPX — the two are often confused. Solid connectors are direct consecrations (by the principal consecrator); dashed connectors mark documented indirect links — co-consecration, organizational succession, or a personnel association — each labeled on the edge and cited. Validity/liceity characterizations are attributed: the Holy See holds these consecrations valid but illicit (gravely unlawful), except the Palmarian line after 1978, which it treats as outside the Church entirely.

Campos line — from Antônio de Castro Mayer (Brazil)

His priests had formed the Priestly Union of Saint John Mary Vianney (15 August 1988); after his death they received their own bishop from SSPX hands, and in 2002 the union reconciled with Rome as the Personal Apostolic Administration Saint John Mary Vianney — the 'other path' of Brazilian traditionalism, often contrasted with the SSPX's.[28] [30] [36]

Palmar de Troya line — from Ngô Đình Thục (NOT SSPX)

Included for disambiguation only: the Palmarian bishops of El Palmar de Troya (Seville, Spain) derive their orders from Archbishop Ngô Đình Thục, not from Lefebvre. There is no episcopal-lineage or organizational connection to the SSPX — but one documented personnel link existed: Fr. Maurice Revaz, canon of the Grand-Saint-Bernard and canon-law professor at Écône, brought Thục to Palmar in December 1975 and was himself consecrated a Palmarian bishop on 28 January 1976 by Clemente Domínguez and others — placing him in the Palmarian, not the Thục, lineage; he left the movement about two years later (Lundberg). Thục's later consecrations also seeded sedevacantist lineages (distinct again from both the SSPX and the Palmarians).[32] [31] [33] [57]

Key figures

Marcel Lefebvre

1905–1991 · France

Founder and first Superior General (1970–1982); former Archbishop of Dakar and Superior General of the Holy Ghost Fathers; suspended a divinis 1976; consecrated four bishops in 1988 and was declared excommunicated; buried at Écône.[3]

Antônio de Castro Mayer

1904–1991 · Brazil

Bishop of Campos, Brazil (coadjutor bishop from 1948, diocesan bishop 1949–1981), the 'other bishop of 1988': co-consecrator of the Écône consecrations. His priests formed the Priestly Union of Saint John Mary Vianney (1988), root of the Campos line that later reconciled with Rome (2002).[28] [30] [36]

Licínio Rangel

1936–2002 · Brazil

Successor of Castro Mayer at the head of the Campos priests' union; consecrated without mandate by three SSPX bishops at São Fidélis (1991); led the union into full communion with Rome as apostolic administrator (January 2002).[29] [30]

Fernando Arêas Rifan

b. 1950 · Brazil

Apostolic administrator of the Campos administration since December 2002; appointed coadjutor by John Paul II and consecrated in full communion (2002).[30]

Franz Schmidberger

b. 1946 · Germany

Superior General 1982–1994; later German district superior and seminary rector.[1]

Bernard Fellay

b. 1958 · Switzerland

One of the four 1988 bishops; Superior General 1994–2018; led the 2000s–2010s negotiations with Rome; elected General Councillor on 20 July 2018 (the 2018 Chapter elected de Galarreta First Assistant, not Fellay); co-consecrator of the 2026 consecrations.[1] [4] [61]

Bernard Tissier de Mallerais

1945–2024 · France

One of the four 1988 bishops; Lefebvre's biographer; principal consecrator of Licínio Rangel for the Campos priests (São Fidélis, 1991); died at Martigny, Switzerland, after a fall, and is buried in the Écône seminary crypt.[1] [51] [52]

Richard Williamson

8 March 1940 – 29 January 2025 · England

One of the four 1988 bishops; expelled from the SSPX in 2012 after public defiance; led the 'Resistance', for which he consecrated six bishops without mandate (2015–2022); his 2009 Holocaust denial caused a worldwide scandal.[5] [27] [54]

Alfonso de Galarreta

b. 1957 · Spain / Argentina

One of the four 1988 bishops; principal consecrator of the 2026 Écône consecrations.[1] [4]

Davide Pagliarani

b. 1970 · Italy

Superior General since 2018 (12-year term); former Italy district superior and rector of La Reja; announced and defended the 2026 consecrations.[6] [4]

François Charrière

1893–1976 · Switzerland

Bishop of Lausanne, Geneva and Fribourg who canonically erected the SSPX in 1970.[1]

Pierre Mamie

1920–2008 · Switzerland

Charrière's successor, who withdrew the Society's canonical approval in 1975.[1]

Paul VI

1897–1978 · Vatican

Pope 1963–1978; publicly rebuked Lefebvre in 1976 and approved the suspension a divinis.[1]

John Paul II

1920–2005 · Vatican

Pope 1978–2005; declared the 1988 consecrations a schismatic act ('Ecclesia Dei') and created the Ecclesia Dei Commission.[13]

Joseph Ratzinger / Benedict XVI

1927–2022 · Vatican

As CDF prefect, negotiated the 1988 protocol; as pope (2005–2013), issued 'Summorum Pontificum' (2007) and remitted the 1988 excommunications (2009).[14] [15]

Francis

1936–2025 · Argentina / Vatican

Pope 2013–2025; granted the SSPX confession faculties (2015/2016) and marriage provisions (2017) while restricting the old rite in the mainstream Church ('Traditionis Custodes', 2021).[16] [18]

Leo XIV (Robert Prevost)

b. 1955 · USA / Peru / Vatican

Pope from May 2025; appealed to the SSPX to desist from the 2026 consecrations, calling them 'a sin of extreme gravity' in his final appeal.[4] [23]

Pascal Schreiber, Michael Goldade, Michel Poinsinet de Sivry, Marc Hanappier

consecrated 2026 · Switzerland / USA / France

The four bishops consecrated at Écône on 1 July 2026 (rectors of the Zaitzkofen and Dillwyn seminaries, the Benelux district superior, and a seminary professor respectively); declared excommunicated by the DDF on 2 July 2026.[4] [23] [25]

Related organizations

FSSP — Priestly Fraternity of Saint Peter

Founded 1988 · Hauterive, Switzerland

Founded on 18 July 1988 by twelve former SSPX priests who accepted 'Ecclesia Dei' after the Écône consecrations; in full communion with Rome; seminaries at Wigratzbad (Germany) and Denton, Nebraska.[8]

Institute of the Good Shepherd (IBP)

Society of Saint Pius V (SSPV)

Founded by nine priests expelled from or leaving the SSPX in a dispute over the liturgy and sedevacantism; sedevacantist-leaning and organizationally separate from the SSPX.[10]

A loose movement — not a single institute — around Bishop Williamson after his 2012 expulsion, rejecting any accord with Rome; Williamson consecrated six bishops without mandate (2015–2022), beginning with Jean-Michel Faure at Nova Friburgo, Brazil (2015).[5]

Priestly Union of Saint John Mary Vianney → Apostolic Administration (Campos)

Disambiguation & nuance

The Society's status and identity are contested terrain. The entries below record distinctions that are easy to flatten; each characterization is attributed to its source.

SSPX is not sedevacantist

The Society has always recognized the reigning popes as legitimate, praying for them in the canon of the Mass, while resisting parts of Vatican II. Sedevacantists (SSPV milieu, CMRI) hold the papal see vacant; the SSPX expelled sedevacantist-leaning priests in 1983.[1] [10]

SSPX ≠ FSSP

Both serve the traditional Latin Mass, but the FSSP was founded in 1988 by priests who left the SSPX precisely to remain in full communion with Rome, and has always been canonically regular.[8]

SSPV ≠ CMRI

Both are US sedevacantist-milieu groups, but they are unrelated organizations; only the SSPV is an SSPX splinter — the CMRI predates the SSPX and never belonged to it.[10] [11]

Canonical status must be dated

1970–1975: approved (pia unio). 1975–2026: canonically irregular — 'no canonical status' in Benedict XVI's 2009 wording — but repeatedly described by Vatican officials as not formally schismatic, with confession and marriage provisions granted 2015–2017. Since 2 July 2026: a DDF decree declares the consecrations schismatic, the six bishops excommunicated and the clergy in schism, and revokes those provisions; the SSPX disputes the decree's validity, and on 11 July 2026 filed a preliminary canonical recourse against it (cann. 1734 ff.), claiming suspensive effect under can. 1353 — the Society's own claim, with no Holy See response reported as of 19 July 2026. Each characterization is attributed to its author.[15] [4] [24] [26] [38] [39]

SSPX ≠ Palmar de Troya

The Palmarian Church of El Palmar de Troya (Spain) is sometimes confused with the SSPX. Its bishops were consecrated in 1976 by Archbishop Ngô Đình Thục — not by Lefebvre — and from 1978 it elected its own popes, placing it outside the Catholic Church entirely; the SSPX has always recognized the Roman pontiff. The one documented personnel connection is Fr. Maurice Revaz, canon-law professor at Écône, who brought Thục to Palmar (December 1975) and became a Palmarian bishop (28 January 1976), leaving about two years later (Lundberg). Lefebvre repudiated the comparison in his 30 June 1988 consecration sermon: there is no question 'of establishing a sort of parallel church as the Bishops of Palmar de Troya have done in Spain… It is out of the question for us to do such things.' See the episcopal genealogy section.[32] [33] [57] [58] [31]
